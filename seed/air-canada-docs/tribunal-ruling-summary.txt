SUMMARY OF RULING
Moffatt v. Air Canada
British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal
Decision: 2024 BCCRT 149
Date of Decision: February 14, 2024

Overview

Mr. Jake Moffatt filed a claim against Air Canada seeking a partial refund of CAD $880 representing the difference between the full fare he paid and the bereavement fare to which he believed he was entitled. The claim arose from an interaction Mr. Moffatt had with Air Canada's website chatbot in November 2022, in which the chatbot informed him he could book at full fare and apply for a bereavement discount retroactively within 90 days of travel.

Air Canada's Position

Air Canada acknowledged that the chatbot had provided incorrect information. However, the airline argued that the chatbot was a separate legal entity responsible for its own actions, and that Air Canada should not be held liable for inaccurate information provided by the chatbot. Air Canada further argued that the correct bereavement fare policy was published on a separate page of the Air Canada website, and that Mr. Moffatt should have consulted that page rather than relying on the chatbot.

Tribunal Findings

The tribunal rejected Air Canada's arguments. The tribunal found that Air Canada is responsible for all information published on its website, including information generated by the chatbot. The tribunal noted that there was no reason for a customer to believe that one part of the Air Canada website (the chatbot) would be less authoritative than another part (the policy page). The tribunal further noted that Air Canada had a duty to take reasonable care to ensure the accuracy of information provided to customers, and that this duty was not discharged by publishing a correct policy elsewhere on the website.

The tribunal ordered Air Canada to pay Mr. Moffatt CAD $650.88 in damages plus CAD $36.14 in pre-judgment interest and CAD $125 in tribunal fees.

Significance

The ruling is widely regarded as one of the first cases in which a court or tribunal held a company legally responsible for the output of an AI chatbot. The decision has been cited in subsequent regulatory discussions about corporate accountability for AI-generated content.
