LAGOS SURGE PRICING CRISIS — TECHNICAL ANALYSIS

Surge Pricing Algorithm Mechanics and Failure Mode

Ride-hailing surge pricing algorithms operate on a geographically partitioned supply-demand model. The city is divided into hexagonal zones (typically using Uber's H3 indexing system or a proprietary equivalent). Each zone maintains a real-time ratio of ride requests to available drivers. When demand exceeds supply beyond a configurable threshold, a multiplier is applied to the base fare. This multiplier increases dynamically as the ratio worsens.

Under normal conditions, surge pricing serves two economic functions: it incentivises drivers to enter high-demand areas by offering higher earnings, and it moderates demand by discouraging price-sensitive riders. In theory, this reaches a market-clearing equilibrium within 10-15 minutes.

The Lagos EndSARS scenario exposed three critical failure modes in this design:

1. SUPPLY COLLAPSE WITHOUT RECOVERY
Standard surge models assume that higher prices will attract drivers into the surge zone. During the Lekki incident, drivers were actively leaving the area due to personal safety concerns. No fare multiplier could compensate for the risk of driving into an active military operation. The algorithm kept raising prices searching for a supply response that could never arrive. In economic terms, the supply curve had become perfectly inelastic — no price would increase the quantity of rides supplied.

2. DEMAND INELASTICITY IN EMERGENCIES
Surge pricing assumes some riders will opt out when prices rise, reducing demand to match available supply. During an emergency evacuation, demand is almost perfectly inelastic — people will pay any price to reach safety. The algorithm interpreted this willingness to pay as a signal to raise prices further, creating a feedback loop where prices spiralled with no moderating mechanism.

3. GEOGRAPHIC SPILLOVER EFFECTS
As the crisis spread across Lagos, surge zones cascaded outward. Drivers leaving Lekki caused supply drops in adjacent zones (Victoria Island, Ikoyi, Surulere), triggering secondary surge events. The algorithm treated each zone independently, failing to recognise that the entire urban system was in crisis.

The technical solution is straightforward but requires deliberate engineering. Several approaches are available:

Emergency Mode Override: A manual or automated trigger that caps the surge multiplier at 1.0x-1.5x during declared emergencies. Uber implemented a version of this in the United States following criticism during Hurricane Sandy in 2012, but it was not consistently deployed in African markets.

Context-Aware Demand Classification: Using external signals — news APIs, government emergency alerts, abnormal cancellation rates, driver safety reports — to classify demand spikes as emergency versus routine. This requires integration with local data sources that may not exist or may not be reliable in all markets.

Geofenced Price Caps: Regulatory requirement for platforms to maintain price ceilings within defined geographic areas during government-declared states of emergency. This shifts the decision from algorithmic to regulatory but introduces latency — governments may not declare emergencies fast enough.

Baseline Fare Guarantee: Rather than capping surge, guarantee that emergency rides are charged at the average fare for that route over the preceding 30 days. This removes the need for real-time classification while ensuring fair pricing.

The Lagos case demonstrates that algorithms optimised for one context (routine urban mobility in developed markets) can cause harm when deployed in contexts with fundamentally different risk profiles. The technical failure was not a bug — the algorithm performed exactly as specified. The failure was in the specification itself, which assumed conditions that do not hold during civil unrest in a megacity with limited public transit alternatives.
