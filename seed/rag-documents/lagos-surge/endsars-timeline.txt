SOURCE DOCUMENT — ILLUSTRATIVE

TIMELINE: THE ENDSARS PROTESTS AND THE LEKKI TOLL GATE INCIDENT
Amnesty International / Premium Times — Compiled December 2020

BACKGROUND

The Special Anti-Robbery Squad (SARS) was a unit of the Nigeria Police Force established in 1992. Over the years, the unit became associated with widespread human rights abuses including extortion, torture, extrajudicial killings, and arbitrary detention. Young Nigerians, particularly those in the technology sector, reported being routinely targeted by SARS officers who would stop them, demand to search their phones and laptops, and extort money based on the assumption that anyone with expensive technology must be involved in internet fraud.

KEY DATES

October 3, 2020: A video circulates on social media appearing to show SARS officers shooting a young man in Delta State. The hashtag #EndSARS trends nationally.

October 8, 2020: Protests spread to Lagos, Abuja, Port Harcourt, and other major cities. Thousands of young Nigerians take to the streets demanding the disbandment of SARS.

October 11, 2020: The Inspector General of Police announces the dissolution of SARS. Protesters remain in the streets, demanding comprehensive police reform.

October 13-19, 2020: Protests continue. The Lekki toll gate in Lagos becomes a major protest site, with demonstrators maintaining a peaceful occupation.

October 20, 2020 — THE LEKKI TOLL GATE INCIDENT

6:00 PM: Lagos State Governor Babajide Sanwo-Olu announces a 24-hour curfew beginning at 9:00 PM.

6:30 PM: The curfew start time is moved forward to 4:00 PM — already past. Thousands of protesters are at the Lekki toll gate with less than no notice to disperse.

6:45 PM: CCTV cameras at the toll gate are removed. Billboards providing lighting to the area are switched off.

6:50 PM: Soldiers from the Nigerian Army arrive at the toll gate.

Approximately 6:50-7:30 PM: Soldiers open fire on the crowd. Multiple eyewitness accounts, video footage, and a subsequent investigation by the Lagos Judicial Panel confirmed live ammunition was used against peaceful, unarmed protesters.

7:00 PM onward: Thousands of Lagosians attempt to flee the Lekki area and surrounding neighbourhoods. Road transportation is chaotic. Ride-hailing apps show surge pricing multipliers of 5-10x as demand for rides overwhelms available supply.

October 21, 2020: Governor Sanwo-Olu claims only one fatality. Amnesty International reports at least 12 people were killed. The exact toll remains disputed.

THE SURGE PRICING CONTEXT

During the hours of October 20 and into October 21, ride-hailing platforms in Lagos experienced extreme demand surges. Their dynamic pricing algorithms, designed to balance supply and demand during routine peak hours, applied standard surge multipliers to a mass displacement event. Users reported fares of N10,000-N15,000 for trips that would normally cost N1,000-N1,500.

The platforms later stated they had "adjusted fares retrospectively" but provided limited details on the scope of refunds or policy changes.
