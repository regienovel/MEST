TRAINING DISTRIBUTION MISMATCH IN MOBILE MONEY FRAUD DETECTION: A CASE STUDY
FSD Kenya Working Paper — March 2024

ABSTRACT

This paper examines the December 2023 M-Pesa false-positive event in which Safaricom's automated fraud detection system incorrectly froze thousands of legitimate small-business accounts during the holiday trading surge. We analyse the incident as a case of training distribution mismatch — a well-documented failure mode in machine learning systems deployed in environments with strong seasonal or contextual variation.

1. BACKGROUND

M-Pesa serves over 30 million active users in Kenya. For millions of informal economy workers — market traders, boda boda operators, small-scale farmers — it is the sole financial infrastructure. Safaricom employs automated fraud detection models that monitor transaction patterns and flag anomalies for account suspension or review.

2. THE INCIDENT

During the period of December 22-28, 2023, an estimated 4,000-8,000 accounts belonging to small business operators were frozen by the automated system. The common profile of affected accounts:
- High transaction volume (3-5x normal daily average)
- Small individual transaction amounts (KES 50-500 per transaction)
- Concentrated activity during market hours (6am-8pm)
- Location clusters around known market areas

All of these patterns are consistent with normal holiday trading behaviour, not fraud.

3. ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS

The fraud detection model appears to define "normal" based on rolling averages of each account's transaction history. When holiday-period activity exceeded the model's learned thresholds, the system classified the divergence as potentially fraudulent.

Key contributing factors:

a) Training data bias: The model's training data likely under-represented extreme seasonal peaks, or treated them as outliers to be excluded.

b) No seasonal adjustment: Unlike traditional banking fraud systems, which incorporate calendar-aware features (holiday periods, payroll cycles, festival seasons), the M-Pesa model appears to have treated all periods uniformly.

c) Informal economy blindness: The model was likely optimised for patterns typical of formal-economy users (salaried individuals with predictable monthly cycles). Informal traders have inherently variable transaction patterns that are poorly captured by standard statistical models.

d) No graduated response: The system applied a binary freeze rather than a graduated response (e.g., flag for review, temporarily limit transaction size, send an SMS verification for large transactions).

4. IMPACT ASSESSMENT

The economic impact on affected traders was disproportionate to the duration of the freeze. For a mama mboga operating on thin daily margins, losing two days of sales during the highest-revenue week of the year can represent 5-10% of annual income. The reputational impact is also significant — customers who experienced failed payments may switch to competing traders.

5. RECOMMENDATIONS

- Incorporate calendar-aware features into the fraud model, with specific attention to culturally significant trading periods (December holidays, Eid, back-to-school January).
- Build user archetypes that reflect informal economy patterns, not just formal-sector banking behaviour.
- Implement graduated responses rather than binary account freezes.
- Provide instant SMS notification when an account is flagged, with a self-service appeal mechanism.
- Establish a rapid-review queue for small-business accounts during known peak periods.
- Conduct regular "seasonal stress tests" on the fraud model using historical peak-period data.
