SOURCE DOCUMENT — ILLUSTRATIVE

"I FELT LIKE A CRIMINAL FOR BEING NIGERIAN": PERSONAL ACCOUNTS OF THE UK VISA STREAMING TOOL
Foxglove / JCWI Joint Report — September 2020

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents testimonies from 42 visa applicants from Nigeria, Ghana, Pakistan, and Bangladesh who were subjected to enhanced scrutiny under the Home Office's algorithmic Streaming Tool between 2017 and 2020. The testimonies reveal a pattern of excessive documentation demands, unexplained delays, and a presumption of guilt that applicants from "green stream" countries rarely experienced.

SELECTED TESTIMONIES

Chioma A., Lagos, Nigeria — Business Visa, 2019
"I was invited to speak at a technology conference in London. I submitted my application with a letter from the conference organisers, proof of my company, bank statements showing savings of over £30,000, and evidence of property ownership in Nigeria. I was asked to attend an interview — something my British colleagues told me they had never heard of for a conference visa. At the interview, the officer asked me repeatedly whether I truly intended to return to Nigeria. My visa took 23 days to process. My colleague from Sweden, attending the same conference, received his in 3 days."

Kwame O., Accra, Ghana — Student Visa, 2018
"I had a full scholarship to a Russell Group university. I submitted every document requested — acceptance letter, scholarship confirmation, bank statements, accommodation proof. I was asked to provide additional evidence of 'ties to Ghana' that would ensure my return. I provided my parents' property deeds, my mother's business registration, and a letter from my church. Even then, the visa took 6 weeks. Students from EU countries on the same programme received theirs in days."

Dr. Amina B., Abuja, Nigeria — Medical Conference, 2020
"I am a consultant cardiologist. I was invited to present research at the Royal College of Physicians. I have travelled to the UK four times before, each time returning to Nigeria as required. Despite this track record, my fifth application was routed for enhanced review. I was asked to provide evidence that my medical practice in Nigeria was 'genuine and subsisting.' I found this deeply insulting."

PATTERNS OBSERVED

Across the 42 testimonies:
- Average processing time: 24 days (versus the standard 15-day service commitment and the 3-7 days typical for green-stream applicants)
- 81% were asked for documentation beyond the standard checklist
- 64% were required to attend an in-person interview
- 31% were initially refused and had to appeal
- Of those who appealed, 85% were ultimately granted their visa — suggesting the initial refusal was unwarranted

CONCLUSION

The Streaming Tool did not merely sort applications by risk. It created a two-tier system in which applicants from certain countries were presumed suspicious until they proved otherwise, while applicants from other countries were presumed legitimate. This inversion of the burden of proof — applied on the basis of nationality — is the essence of algorithmic discrimination.
