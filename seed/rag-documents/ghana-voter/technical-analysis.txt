SOURCE DOCUMENT — ILLUSTRATIVE

FINGERPRINT RECOGNITION FAILURES IN WEST AFRICAN ELECTORAL CONTEXTS: A TECHNICAL REVIEW
Journal of African Elections, Vol. 21, No. 1 — June 2022

ABSTRACT

This paper examines the persistent problem of fingerprint biometric verification failures in West African elections, with a focus on Ghana's experience in the 2012-2020 electoral cycles. We identify the technical, demographic, and environmental factors that contribute to high false rejection rates among specific populations and propose design principles for more inclusive biometric systems.

1. THE FINGERPRINT QUALITY PROBLEM

Fingerprint recognition systems rely on detecting and matching minutiae — the unique ridge endings and bifurcations in a person's fingerprint. The quality of a fingerprint capture depends on the clarity and distinctness of these ridge patterns.

Several well-documented factors degrade fingerprint quality:

a) Occupational wear: Manual labour — particularly farming, fishing, construction, and market trading — physically wears down the ridges of the fingerprint over time. This effect is cumulative and largely irreversible. Studies in South Asian and African agricultural communities have found that 30-40% of adults over age 50 who have spent their careers in manual labour have fingerprints that fall below the quality threshold for reliable automated matching.

b) Skin moisture: Very dry skin (common in harmattan season in West Africa) produces faint fingerprint images. Very wet or oily skin can cause smudging. Both reduce matching accuracy.

c) Age: Ridge definition naturally decreases with age due to loss of skin elasticity.

d) Scarring and skin conditions: Cuts, burns, and dermatological conditions can permanently alter fingerprint patterns.

2. THE GENDERED DIMENSION

The intersection of occupation and gender produces a distinctly gendered pattern of biometric exclusion. In Ghana, women dominate the agricultural and market trading workforce:

- Women constitute approximately 52% of the agricultural labour force
- Women comprise the vast majority of market traders (the "mama mboga" or "market woman" archetype)
- Women's agricultural and domestic work — including pounding fufu, washing clothes by hand, and preparing food — involves sustained friction, heat, and chemical exposure that degrades fingerprints more rapidly than many forms of male-dominated manual labour

The result: older women in rural and peri-urban communities experience the highest rates of biometric verification failure.

3. TESTING GAP

A critical factor in the Ghana 2020 failures was the testing methodology used by the Electoral Commission. The BVDs were tested primarily in controlled environments using younger, urban volunteers. The testing population did not adequately represent the demographic groups most vulnerable to verification failure.

Had the EC conducted field testing in farming communities with older women — the population with the highest known risk of fingerprint degradation — the failure rates would have been identified before election day.

4. RECOMMENDATIONS

For election management bodies deploying biometric verification:

a) Multi-modal biometrics: Supplement fingerprint verification with facial recognition or iris scanning, providing fallback authentication methods that do not depend on fingerprint quality.

b) Population-representative testing: Test biometric systems with demographically representative samples, explicitly including populations known to have degraded fingerprints.

c) Clear manual override protocols: Establish and train poll workers on unambiguous override procedures, so that technology failure does not become voter disenfranchisement.

d) Quality scoring at registration: Flag registrants whose fingerprint quality scores fall below a reliability threshold and proactively provide them with alternative verification methods.
