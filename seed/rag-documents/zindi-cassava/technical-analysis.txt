SOURCE DOCUMENT — ILLUSTRATIVE

DATASET-DEPLOYMENT GAP IN AGRICULTURAL AI: LESSONS FROM CASSAVA DISEASE CLASSIFICATION
Proceedings of the African Conference on Machine Learning — November 2022

ABSTRACT

We present a systematic analysis of why a cassava disease classification model that achieved 95% accuracy on a competition test set performed below 40% when deployed via a WhatsApp-based interface with farmers in northern Ghana. We identify three categories of failure — distribution shift, spurious correlation, and confidence calibration — and propose guidelines for agricultural AI deployment in sub-Saharan Africa.

1. THE COMPETITION DATASET

The training dataset consisted of approximately 21,000 images of cassava leaves photographed in controlled conditions:
- Camera: DSLR or high-quality smartphone cameras
- Lighting: Natural daylight, consistent exposure
- Background: Typically a hand holding the leaf, or the leaf placed on a neutral surface
- Resolution: 800x600 pixels minimum
- Labelling: Expert-annotated by plant pathologists

2. THE DEPLOYMENT ENVIRONMENT

Images submitted by farmers via WhatsApp were captured under markedly different conditions:
- Camera: Low-to-mid-range smartphone cameras, often with scratched lenses
- Lighting: Variable — harsh midday sun, overcast skies, late afternoon shadow
- Background: Leaves photographed in situ on the plant, with soil, other plants, sky, and human hands visible
- Resolution: Variable, often compressed by WhatsApp
- Framing: Leaves partially obscured, multiple leaves in frame, variable distance

3. SPURIOUS CORRELATION ANALYSIS

Using gradient-weighted class activation mapping (Grad-CAM), we visualised what regions of the image the model attended to when making classifications. Key findings:

- For 34% of correctly classified competition test images, the model's highest-activation regions were in the background or at image edges, not on the leaf itself
- The five disease categories in the training set had statistically different background colour distributions (p < 0.01), suggesting images for different categories were photographed in systematically different settings
- When we artificially replaced the backgrounds of test images with random textures, the model's accuracy dropped from 95% to 71% — confirming that background features contributed significantly to classification

4. CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION FAILURE

The model was not only inaccurate on field images but confidently inaccurate:
- Mean confidence score on correct classifications: 0.87
- Mean confidence score on incorrect classifications: 0.79

The model expressed high confidence even when wrong, providing no signal to the user or to the system that its predictions should be treated with caution. A well-calibrated model would express lower confidence when presented with out-of-distribution inputs.

5. RECOMMENDATIONS

For researchers and practitioners deploying agricultural AI:

a) Train on deployment-representative data. Competition datasets are useful for benchmarking but insufficient for deployment. Collect additional training images using the same devices, in the same conditions, as the intended deployment environment.

b) Test for spurious correlations. Apply interpretability techniques (Grad-CAM, LIME, SHAP) to verify the model is attending to disease-relevant features, not background artifacts.

c) Implement confidence thresholds. Set a minimum confidence threshold below which the model declines to classify and instead recommends consulting an extension officer.

d) Include a "none of the above" class. If the image quality is too poor for reliable classification, or the image does not clearly show a cassava leaf, the model should say so.

e) Test with real users before deployment. A pilot with even 20 farmers using their own phones would have revealed the accuracy gap before the system was launched.
