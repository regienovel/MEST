BRIDGING THE GAP: FROM COMPETITION ACCURACY TO FIELD ACCURACY IN AFRICAN AGRITECH AI
Zindi Blog — Guest Post by Dr. Abena Owusu, KNUST — April 2022

Zindi competitions have produced some impressive machine learning models for African challenges — from crop disease detection to wildlife conservation to financial inclusion. But the cassava disease classification experience in northern Ghana exposed a truth our community must confront: competition accuracy is not deployment accuracy.

THE PROMISE

Cassava is the primary food security crop for over 800 million people globally and is critical across West Africa. Disease accounts for annual losses estimated at $1-2 billion across the continent. Early detection allows farmers to remove infected plants before disease spreads, potentially saving entire harvests.

When the winning Zindi model hit 95% accuracy, there was genuine excitement. Here was a tool that could put expert-level disease diagnosis in every farmer's pocket via WhatsApp — no extension officer visit required, no laboratory testing, just point your phone and get an answer.

THE REALITY

When 200 farmers in the Upper East Region of Ghana used the WhatsApp-based tool over three months, the model correctly diagnosed fewer than 40% of submissions. Worse, many of the incorrect diagnoses were confidently wrong — the model might declare a healthy leaf to be severely diseased, prompting a farmer to destroy healthy plants.

After a few weeks, most farmers stopped using the tool. "If it's wrong more than it's right, what's the point?" one farmer told us.

THE ROOT CAUSES

Our post-mortem identified three interlocking problems:

1. The training data didn't look like reality. Competition images were high quality, well lit, and carefully framed. Farmer photos were taken on cheap phones in bright sun with messy backgrounds.

2. The model learned shortcuts. Instead of learning what cassava disease actually looks like, the model partially learned what the training photos looked like — including their backgrounds, lighting, and framing conventions.

3. Nobody tested with real farmers first. The model went from competition leaderboard to deployment without anyone sending a photo from a real farmer's phone to see if it still worked.

WHAT WE LEARNED

The experience taught us several lessons that apply to any AI system being deployed in African contexts:

First, the people who build the model must visit the field. Our data scientists had never been to a cassava farm in the Upper East Region. If they had, they would have immediately noticed the gap between training data and reality.

Second, confidence scores matter as much as predictions. A model that says "I'm not sure — please consult an extension officer" is more useful than one that confidently gives the wrong answer. We should have set a confidence threshold below which the model refused to classify.

Third, competitions should include a "deployment track." Zindi and similar platforms could require winning teams to demonstrate that their models work on data collected in realistic field conditions, not just on held-out test data from the same distribution.

We are now rebuilding the cassava tool with images collected by farmers themselves, using their own phones, in their own fields. Early results are promising — accuracy is lower than the competition model on competition data, but dramatically higher on real-world data. That's the only metric that matters.
