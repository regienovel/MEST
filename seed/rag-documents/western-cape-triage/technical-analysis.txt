WESTERN CAPE HEALTHCARE TRIAGE MODEL — TECHNICAL ANALYSIS

Algorithmic Bias in Postcode-Proxied Socioeconomic Features

The Western Cape triage model was a gradient-boosted decision tree (XGBoost) trained on approximately 180,000 emergency department encounters from 2018-2021 across eight district hospitals. The target variable was a composite outcome score reflecting 30-day mortality, ICU admission, and hospital readmission within 7 days. The model was intended to predict which patients would benefit most from early specialist intervention.

Feature engineering included 47 input variables grouped into four categories: clinical presentation (vital signs, complaint codes, Glasgow Coma Scale), patient demographics (age, sex, recorded comorbidities), healthcare utilisation history (number of prior ED visits, appointment adherence rate, time since last visit), and geographic indicators (residential postcode, distance to facility, facility load at time of presentation).

The inclusion of postcode as a feature was the primary vector for bias introduction. In the Western Cape, residential postcode is a near-perfect proxy for race and socioeconomic status, a legacy of apartheid-era spatial planning that persists in contemporary settlement patterns. Khayelitsha (7784), Gugulethu (7750), Mitchells Plain (7785), and Nyanga (7764) are predominantly Black African and Coloured communities with lower average incomes, while Bellville (7530), Durbanville (7550), and the Southern Suburbs (7700-series) are wealthier and historically white.

Feature importance analysis showed that postcode ranked as the 6th most influential variable in the model, ahead of several clinical indicators. When postcode was removed, the model's AUC dropped by only 0.02 (from 0.81 to 0.79), suggesting it contributed marginal predictive value while introducing substantial fairness costs.

The utilisation history features compounded the problem. Appointment adherence rate — the percentage of scheduled follow-up appointments a patient actually attended — was the 3rd most important feature. Patients from township postcodes had an average adherence rate of 47%, compared to 78% for suburban patients. This gap reflected transport barriers (a return trip from Khayelitsha to Tygerberg Hospital can cost R120 and take 3 hours by public transit), inflexible employment (many township residents work in informal or hourly-paid jobs without sick leave), and clinic-side factors (average wait times at township health centres exceed 4 hours).

The model treated low adherence as a patient-level risk factor indicating lower likelihood of benefiting from specialist referral. In reality, it was a system-level access barrier that the healthcare system itself had failed to address. By encoding this barrier as a patient characteristic, the model effectively punished patients for being poor.

Recommended technical remediation includes: (1) removing postcode and all geographic features from the model entirely, (2) replacing appointment adherence with clinically-derived acuity scores only, (3) implementing fairness constraints during training that enforce equal Tier 1 assignment rates across postcode groups for patients with equivalent clinical presentations, and (4) requiring human clinician review for all Tier 2 and Tier 3 assignments, with the algorithmic score presented as advisory only, never determinative.

The model was never intended to discriminate. Its developers at a Cape Town health-tech startup had tested for accuracy but not for equity. They had measured aggregate performance (AUC, precision, recall) without stratifying by postcode or socioeconomic group. This is a common failure pattern in health AI — optimising for average performance while allowing disparate impact on vulnerable subgroups.
