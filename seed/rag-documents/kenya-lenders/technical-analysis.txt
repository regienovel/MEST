SOURCE DOCUMENT — ILLUSTRATIVE

SPEED WITHOUT SAFEGUARDS: THE ALGORITHMIC ARCHITECTURE OF DIGITAL LENDING IN EAST AFRICA
FSD Africa — Research Report, June 2022

1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Digital lending apps in Kenya approve microloans in as little as 7 seconds using machine learning models that analyse smartphone data as a proxy for creditworthiness. This paper examines the algorithmic architecture of these lending systems, identifies where human oversight was systematically removed, and analyses the consequences for borrowers.

2. THE DATA PIPELINE

Digital lending apps typically request the following permissions upon installation:
- Contacts: Full access to the user's phone contacts
- SMS: Ability to read incoming and outgoing text messages
- Call logs: Record of all incoming and outgoing calls
- Location: GPS tracking of the user's movements
- Device information: Phone model, operating system, storage usage, installed apps
- Calendar: Access to scheduled events

From this data, the lending algorithm extracts hundreds of features, including:
- Communication patterns: Frequency and diversity of contacts, ratio of incoming to outgoing calls
- Financial signals: M-Pesa transaction notifications in SMS history, salary credit patterns
- Behavioural stability: Regularity of daily routines inferred from location data, consistency of phone charging patterns
- Social graph: Size and composition of contact network, frequency of communication with "high-value" contacts

3. THE CREDIT DECISION

The model assigns a credit score based on these features and makes a binary decision: approve or decline. If approved, the loan amount is determined by the score, with first-time borrowers typically receiving KES 500-2,000 and amounts increasing with successful repayment.

The entire process — from data collection to credit decision to disbursement — happens in seconds, with no human review. This speed is a core value proposition: unlike traditional banking, which might take days or weeks to process a loan application, digital lenders offer instant gratification.

4. THE COLLECTION ALGORITHM

The same algorithmic approach extends to collections. When a loan becomes overdue, an automated escalation sequence activates:

Day 1 overdue: Push notification reminding the borrower of the payment
Day 2-3: SMS messages with increasingly urgent language
Day 4-7: Automated calls with pre-recorded messages
Day 7-14: SMS messages sent to the borrower's emergency contacts
Day 14+: In some apps, SMS messages sent to the borrower's full contact list

The final step — mass SMS to contacts — is the practice known as "digital shaming." It was designed as a collection mechanism exploiting social pressure in cultures where financial reputation is deeply tied to community standing.

5. WHERE THE HUMAN DISAPPEARED

The critical design choice in these systems is the systematic removal of human judgment from every stage:

- No human reviews the loan application
- No human assesses whether the borrower can afford to repay
- No human reviews whether the interest rate is appropriate for the borrower's circumstances
- No human decides when to escalate collection efforts
- No human evaluates whether the borrower is experiencing genuine hardship
- No human considers whether contacting the borrower's family, employer, or pastor is proportionate

Each of these decisions, made by a human, would involve weighing competing considerations: the lender's right to recover funds versus the borrower's dignity, privacy, and wellbeing. The algorithm weighs only one factor: maximising repayment probability.

6. THE HUMAN COST

The consequences of this design have been severe:
- Microsave Consulting found that 40% of digital borrowers in Kenya reported reducing food consumption to repay digital loans
- The Kenya National Bureau of Statistics reported that digital loan default-related stress was cited in a growing number of mental health presentations at public health facilities
- Multiple suicides have been attributed to digital shaming practices in Kenyan media, though comprehensive data is not available

7. LESSONS

The digital lending crisis in Kenya demonstrates that speed and scale without human oversight creates systems that are efficient at extraction but incapable of mercy. The algorithm can process a loan in 7 seconds, but it cannot spend even 1 second considering whether it should.
